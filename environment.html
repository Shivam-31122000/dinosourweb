Triassic Period (252-201 million years ago)

The Triassic period marked the beginning of the Mesozoic Era. The climate was generally hot and dry, with vast deserts covering much of Pangaea - the supercontinent that contained all of Earth's landmass.

Seasonal monsoons occurred in the tropics, and there were no polar ice caps. The first dinosaurs appeared during this period, along with the first mammals.

Vegetation was dominated by ferns, cycads, and conifers. Flowering plants had not yet evolved. The supercontinent Pangaea began to show the first signs of breaking apart, with rift valleys forming that would eventually become the Atlantic Ocean.

Marine life included the first ichthyosaurs and nothosaurs, while on land, early archosaurs and therapsids dominated before the rise of true dinosaurs.

Jurassic Period (201-145 million years ago)

During the Jurassic, Pangaea began to break apart into Laurasia (north) and Gondwana (south). The climate became more humid and tropical worldwide, with higher oxygen levels supporting the growth of giant insects and massive dinosaurs.

Lush forests covered the land, with ferns, cycads, and conifers creating dense vegetation that supported enormous herbivorous dinosaurs. The first birds appeared, evolving from small theropod dinosaurs, marking one of the most significant evolutionary transitions.

Sea levels rose significantly, creating extensive shallow seas that covered parts of the continents. These warm, nutrient-rich waters teemed with marine reptiles like plesiosaurs and ichthyosaurs, while ammonites diversified into numerous species.

This period saw the dominance of giant sauropods like Brachiosaurus and Diplodocus, which evolved long necks to reach high vegetation. Stegosaurs developed their distinctive plates and spikes for defense and thermoregulation.

The breakup of continents created isolated environments that accelerated evolutionary diversification. Volcanic activity was widespread, contributing to the rich mineral deposits that would later become valuable resources.

Coastal areas featured extensive coral reefs, and the first lizards and modern-type mammals appeared, though they remained small and nocturnal to avoid predation by dinosaurs.

Cretaceous Period (145-66 million years ago)

The Cretaceous saw the continents moving toward their modern positions. The climate was warmer than today, with high sea levels creating extensive shallow inland seas like the Western Interior Seaway that divided North America.

Flowering plants (angiosperms) appeared and diversified rapidly, revolutionizing terrestrial ecosystems. This period featured the most famous dinosaurs like T-Rex, Triceratops, and Velociraptor, which developed increasingly sophisticated hunting and defense strategies.

The period ended with the Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction event, likely caused by an asteroid impact combined with massive volcanic activity, which wiped out all non-avian dinosaurs and about 75% of all species on Earth.

Marine ecosystems flourished with mosasaurs becoming the dominant predators, while pterosaurs reached their largest sizes with wingspans exceeding 10 meters. Social behavior in dinosaurs became more complex, with evidence of herding, nesting colonies, and parental care.

Lush Forests

Dense forests of conifers, ferns, and cycads covered much of the land, providing habitat and food for herbivorous dinosaurs. These forests created complex ecosystems with multiple canopy layers.

Warm Seas

Shallow inland seas teemed with marine reptiles, ammonites, and early fish species during the Mesozoic era. These warm waters supported diverse coral reefs and plankton blooms.

Active Geology

Continental drift and mountain building created diverse landscapes and isolated ecosystems for evolution. Volcanic activity enriched soils and shaped the atmosphere.

Continental Drift During Mesozoic

The breakup of Pangaea during the Mesozoic Era dramatically changed Earth's geography and climate, creating new ocean currents and weather patterns that influenced evolution
